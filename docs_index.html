Spark-CTGS Consumer Transparency Governance Standard • v1.0

If the system profits from your confusion,
it’s already broken.

Spark-CTGS defines how consumer-facing systems must behave when products, platforms, and service stacks start failing in recursive, opaque ways.

Consumers aren’t the test bench. They’re the boundary. CTGS treats repeated failure loops, hidden cost escalations, and opaque flows of money and control as governance problems, not “user error” or “edge cases.”

What CTGS focuses on

CTGS is a governance standard for systems that quietly turn user confusion, friction, and lock-in into a revenue model.

  • Recursive troubleshooting loops that never resolve
  • Compounding “fix” costs that exceed product value
  • Hidden ecosystem locks and incompatibilities
  • Float capture during refunds, upgrades, and disputes
  • AI recommenders that steer users into known failure patterns

How implementers should use CTGS

CTGS is designed for organizations that design, operate, or audit complex consumer-facing systems: platforms, device ecosystems, financial flows, and AI-mediated interfaces.

  • Use Standard_v1.0.md to align your internal policies and product requirements with CTGS rights and obligations.
  • Use Implementation_Notes_v1.0.md to map CTGS concepts onto real workflows: support flows, upgrade paths, refund logic, recommender behavior, and long-tail failure cases.
  • Treat HASHES.md and NOTARIZATION.md as the record of what CTGS v1.0 said at publication time.
CTGS does not replace local law or sector-specific regulation. It gives you a neutral, implementation-ready baseline for consumer transparency and harm-pattern governance.

Relationship to other governance work

Spark-CTGS is intended to sit alongside other standards and governance frameworks, not to replace them.

  • It can be used as a transparency and harm-pattern layer on top of existing security, privacy, and safety regimes.
  • It is technology-neutral and does not assume any specific programming language, platform, or AI architecture.
  • It is suitable for regulators, auditors, and engineering teams who need a shared vocabulary for “harmful recursion” in consumer-facing systems.

Versioning & integrity

CTGS v1.0 is treated as an immutable release. Any future revisions (for example, v1.1 or v2.0) should:

  • Clearly state what changed and why.
  • Regenerate and publish new file hashes.
  • Anchor new manifests via an auditable notarization process.

The current integrity chain is described in:

  • HASHES.md — per-file SHA-256 hashes.
  • NOTARIZATION.md — manifest hash, timestamp, and anchor details.
  • HASHES.md.ots — OpenTimestamps proof (Bitcoin-backed timestamp).
Consumers should be able to verify that the version of CTGS they are reading is the same one that was originally published.

Why CTGS exists

CTGS exists for the scenarios where “support,” “loyalty,” and “ecosystem value” have quietly turned into:

  • Endless reset / reinstall / retry loops that never resolve.
  • “Upgrade” offers that only appear after enough failure.
  • Compatibility gaps that are invisible until after purchase.
  • Opaque refund / chargeback flows where the float is monetized.
  • AI-driven flows that steer confused users toward more spend.
CTGS treats those patterns as governance failures, not as acceptable business models. Systems that profit from confusion are not “working as intended.”
